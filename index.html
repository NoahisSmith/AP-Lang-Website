Fahrenheit 451

This book takes place in a futuristic world filled with advanced technology, violence, media control, and manipulation. In this world, the population is plagued with instant media, gratification, and pleasure. Citizens heavily consume pills and disregard their health. Society no longer wants to learn of the world around them and only live in the present. The Protagonist, Guy Montag, is a fireman whose job is to burn and destroy books as a way of censorship of the past. He soon begins to question this way of life and the world around him...

Major Events:

The Fireman

Guy Montag is a fireman who loves his job. He is in charge of burning any literature discovered in the area and describes it as “a special pleasure to see things blackened and changed” and as “This great python spitting its venomous kerosene upon the world”

Clarisse

Montag meets a girl named Clarisse one day while walking home. She is very interested in nature and asks him many questions about himself. At first, Montag thinks she is odd but later develops a genuine relationship with her, something very rare in this society. He begins to question the lifestyle he lives and whether or not he is truly happy with his life.

Overdose

After returning home from work one-day Montag discovers his wife overdosed on sleeping pills. He quickly rushes her to the hospital but instead of finding a doctor he is greeted by two plumbers smoking a cigarette. They take his wife and lazily suck the contents of her stomach out using a snake-like tube. So many cases of overdose happen in this city that they no longer use doctors but untrained plumbers instead. Montag is stunned and develops even more distrust in society and the world around him. He finds he is not happy with this way of life.

Professor Faber

In the pursuit of answers, Montag meets with an old professor to discuss literature and how to understand the meaning of books. He begins to steal books from his job and read stories. Faber helps him understand the meaning and serves as a mentor for Montag. He also informs him of an approching atomic war that's about to strike the area.

Reading

Montag begins to call in sick to work and instead reads his stolen books. He tries to convince his wife that their way of life is not fulfilling and that she must look deeper into the world around her and begin to question society. She refuses and ignores him. One day while his wife is gossiping with her friends, he overhears their selfish and meaningless conversation. This angers him and he begins to argue and read poetry out loud. The women get upset and flee the house crying. Montag's boss, Beatty, hears of this incident and is not happy.

The Burning

Montag returns to work and answers a call to burn a house in suspicion of harboring books. Upon arriving at the destination, he soon realizes that the house in front of him is, in fact, his own. He is instructed by Beatty to burn all of his possessions and belongings. Beatty begins to verbally abuse Montag and threatens to kill Faber and have him locked in jail. Montag warns Beatty to stop but he continues to threaten him. Montag then burns Beatty alive in a climatic blaze.

The Escape

News of this event begins to spread and a search for Montag begins. Major media coverage of this investigation ensues and Montag races out of the city as a deadly mechanical hound follows his chemical trail. Montag stops at Faber's house to borrows some clothes and says his goodbyes before leaving the city. Faber gives him some advice on where to go and they both part ways.

A new begining

Montag successfully escapes the city and looses the mechanical hound and authorities. He wonders the wooded countryside and discovers a group of intellectuals who fled for similar purposes. They explain to Montag that their new purpose in life is to memorize books and one day teach the world the lessons they hold. While traveling the area they witness the beginning and end of the atomic war. The city and everything inside is wiped into oblivion and all that remains is the rubble and ash. Montag decides to join the group and dedicate his life to rebuilding a literate and cultural society.

The author's message

The author of Fahrenheit 451, Ray Bradbury, warns the audience of the dangers of censorship. This society heavily consumes pills, drugs, media, drive fast and recklessly and live faced paced lives. Bradbury shows that a society with too much stimulation doesn't have enough time to stop and think. This allows for a society to believe books are too overwhelming of a task to stop and think about. This also allows individuals to envy others with greater knowledge and become hostile towards them. A society of this nature mask their self-hatred and sadness with instant gratification and harmful pleasures. Through these examples, Bradbury warns us of the dangers of censorship.
